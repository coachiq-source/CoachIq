vs PC

April 26 HOME NON-LEAGUE
Full turf, 110 x 60 yards — artificial surface
Their System

Defensive Base

Man-to-man — they press the ball carrier everywhere on the field. Expect tight on-ball pressure from the moment you catch the clear. Their defense is built to deny easy outlets and force turnovers under pressure.

Offensive Identity

Inside game — their crease attack is dominant. They will feed the crease relentlessly and look to finish close-range. Expect dodges from up top to set up inside feeds, with secondary looks coming from two-man games around X.

EMO danger: Medium-high (4/5) — They run a disciplined EMO set with a patient approach. Their crease finisher is their primary look, with skip passes to the wings as the release valve. Expect them to probe for 20+ seconds before forcing a shot.
Goalie Tendencies
  • Strong shot-reader — he tracks the ball deep and closes angles early. Fake-shoot-then-relocate will beat his early commitment.
  • Shuts down close range — his positioning at the crease is elite. Take him from distance first to test his range; if he's covering the pipes, go low and hard off-hip.
  • Hesitation shot forces him to drop his stick — he anticipates the release. Pump fake high, shoot low on the recovery.
  • Expect quick outlet on saves — he's an outlet-starter. Wings need to check their slide man before the shot; he will look long the second he sees clear ice.
  • Test him early with a bad-angle wing shot — we need a read on whether he drops his stick early or tracks the ball late into his hip.
Top Threats

John / #1 — FOGO

"Face-off specialist — wins possession every draw."

How to Defend

  • Wings read his clamp. If he clamps, counter-body immediately; if he rakes, split forward and scoop ahead of him.
  • Don't concede the ground ball — #1 is winning possession, not the 50/50. Your wings need to be aggressive on the release, not passive.
  • After he wins, expect a quick push to their offensive end — your clear unit needs to be in position before he crosses midfield.
Your match-up: Your best face-off midfielder takes the draw. Wings are your two fastest ground-ball players — they own the scoop battle, not the clamp race.

Your Offensive Answer

What to Run

Primary: Motion offense with quick ball movement — against their tight man-to-man press, you need to move the ball faster than they can shift. Skip passes and quick releases punish over-aggressive slides. Change-up: Invert and two-man game from X. If their defense locks off the perimeter, send an attackman up top and run a midfielder behind — their defense will be caught between chasing the ball and protecting the crease.

Face-off Keys

Expect to lose the possession battle 60-40 or worse — #1 is dominant. Wings go for denial, not the ground ball; force the 50/50 and be disciplined on the recovery. Every possession you win is a bonus; do not give it away on a bad clear.

Clearing Key

Critical adjustment from intake: Tighten your clearing — you turned it over under pressure last time out. Against their 10-man ride: goalie to the crease defender, first pass up-field to the strong-side midfielder, second look is the skip to the weak-side wing. If the first outlet is covered, swing it back through the goalie and reset. Do not force a pass into traffic — their defense is built to capitalize on turnovers under pressure.

EMD (Man-Down) Game Plan
EMD shape: Disciplined rotating box-plus-one, with scout-specific denial on their crease finisher. Double-team the inside feed; force them to shoot from outside.
  • Protect the crease — their crease attack is their primary EMO look. Adjacent defender sinks to deny the feed; do not chase the ball-carrier and leave the crease open.
  • Communicate the skip — when the ball moves wing-to-wing, everybody rotates one spot and calls it out loud. No silent rotations.
  • Force the outside shot — accept that they will get a shot off; the job is to make it low-percentage (outside, strong-hand-covered, feet moving).
Timeout Scripts

Timeout A — Protecting a Lead

"We're up. That's because we're executing. Don't change anything. Burn 30 seconds on this next possession — make them chase. Your longpole at the crease has been dominant — rest him for 90 seconds, then bring him back in for their next possession. Do your job. Next whistle."

Timeout B — Chasing a Deficit

"We're not playing our game. We're turning it over on clears under their press. Here's the fix: goalie swings it back if the first outlet is covered — do not force the pass. On offense, we're settling for outside shots when their goalie is shutting that down — go inside, feed the crease, make them slide. Two ground balls. Two goals. Go."

Halftime Adjustment Triggers
  • IF their crease attack has scored twice by halftime → switch to body-first-no-stick-checks on the crease starting possession 1 of Q3; adjacent defender plays adjacent slide and does NOT leave the crease unmarked.
  • IF you're losing the face-off / draw battle 70-30 or worse → send the wings in denial mode on the scoop, accept the turnover if necessary, and re-ride the clear. Do not let them walk it up uncontested after #1 wins the draw.
  • IF you're turning it over on clears more than twice per quarter → slow down the outlet. Goalie holds for 3 seconds, defenders spread wide to the restraining line, and the first pass is a safe dump to the crease defender. No long outlets until the ride pressure drops.
  • IF their goalie has saved more than 60% of your outside shots → go exclusively inside-finisher dodges for two possessions. Attackmen drive from X, midfielders cut to the crease, force him to defend the crease lane instead of sitting back and reading your release.
  • IF your longpole picks up two personals → slide your SSDM to longpole coverage on their crease attack and move your second longpole to SSDM. Do not leave their crease finisher with a short-stick matchup.
Field Notes
  • Full turf, artificial surface — ground balls will skid on the turf, especially if it's been wet down before the game. Get low, stop the ball with your body before scooping.
  • 110 x 60 yards (regulation field) — transition lanes are full-length. Clearing passes should be chest-level and in-stride, not floated skip passes that give their ride time to recover.
  • Home game — you control the sideline noise. Use hand signals from the bench for EMD rotations; vocal calls will be drowned out when their parents are loud.
  • April 26 start — expect late-afternoon sun if the game is at 4pm or later. Check the field orientation during warm-up; if you're shooting into the sun in Q2/Q4, shooters wear visors and take the south cage at warm-up to test depth of field on high shots.
  • Non-league game, first time playing PC this season — treat this as a scouting opportunity. Their crease attack and #1's face-off dominance are the two things to log for future matchups. If you see them again in the playoffs, you'll have the read.
